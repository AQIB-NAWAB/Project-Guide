# 10.1 — Set the scene

**Where the project is now:** Chapter 9 gave vendors a **store API** — create, fetch mine, update — proven with curl. A logged-in vendor can open a shop programmatically. The React app still treats every vendor like any other authenticated user: no shop setup screen, no settings page, no gate blocking dashboard routes.

**Where this chapter goes:** Vendors **open and manage their shop in the browser** — setup form, settings form, and an onboarding redirect that forces storeless vendors through `/vendor/open-shop` before they reach product pages.

This is a **build chapter**. Every sub-chapter from 10.5 onward adds client routes and pages. The gate is **runnable proof**: new vendor registers → forced to open shop → can edit settings → cannot reach product routes until store exists.

---

## Bridge from last chapter

Chapter 9 gave vendors a **store API** — `POST /stores`, `GET /stores/me`, `PATCH /stores/:id` — all proven with curl and protected by Chapter 8 middleware. A developer can open a shop; a **human vendor** still sees the same generic React app as everyone else. Chapter 7 taught the login UI pattern; Chapter 10 applies it to **vendor onboarding**: forms, settings, and a redirect gate that sends storeless vendors to "Open your shop" before they reach inventory routes.

| Term | Simple meaning |

## Think of it like a shop lease signing

Chapter 9 built the **registry office** where stall applications are filed. Chapter 10 is the **counter where the vendor actually signs** — a form in the browser, not a curl command. If they try to walk into the stockroom (`/vendor/products`) before signing, security sends them back to the lease desk. Same API underneath; humans get screens.

## Common beginner questions

**Q: Why another frontend chapter — didn't Chapter 7 cover React?**  
Chapter 7 was **auth for everyone**. Chapter 10 is **vendor-specific flows** — different routes, different gate, different API helpers (`stores.js`).

**Q: Can I build product pages here since the vendor is already logged in?**  
No. Stay on **shop identity only**. Products are Chapters 11 (API) and 12 (UI). Mixing them makes debugging harder.

**Q: What happens when `GET /stores/me` returns 404?**  
Redirect to `/vendor/open-shop` (or your chosen path). That is the core onboarding story — not an error page.

**Q: Do customers see these pages?**  
No. `/vendor/*` routes are for sellers. Customers get their own shop experience in Week 3.

**Q: Do I need new server endpoints?**  
Usually no — Chapter 9's API is enough. This chapter is **client-only** unless you discover a gap during testing.

## The onboarding story (user narrative)

1. Maya registers as vendor, verifies OTP, logs in.  
2. App checks `GET /stores/me` → **404** — no shop yet.  
3. Router sends her to **Open your shop** — name, description, address.  
4. Submit → `POST /stores` → **201**.  
5. Redirect to vendor dashboard home (product list comes in Ch 12).  
6. Later she edits blurb in **Store settings** → `PATCH /stores/:id`.  

If she bookmarks `/vendor/products` before step 4, the **gate** sends her back to open-shop.
