# 1.3 — The product and its people

Before you meet the vendors and customers, meet the **product** — the thing you're building, described the way a user would experience it, not the way a system diagram would decompose it.

Imagine a Saturday morning. Someone opens your app on their phone. They're not buying from one supermarket — they're buying from **every local shop in the neighbourhood**, in one session. They add two kilograms of basmati rice from **Ali's Grocer**, a bunch of coriander from **Green Valley Farm**, and a bottle of cold-pressed oil from **The Pantry**. One cart. One checkout button. Behind that button, your platform does the hard work: it splits the purchase into **three separate orders**, notifies each shop of their slice, and locks the prices on each receipt so a price change next week never rewrites what they paid today.

That's the product. Everything in this course serves that experience.

---

## The product in one sentence

**A local grocery marketplace where independent shops sell through one platform, customers shop across all of them in a single cart and checkout, and every vendor sees only their own business.**

Name it yourself. Run it in a clean, editorial visual style — generous whitespace, readable type, product photography doing the selling. The course cares about behaviour and structure, not your exact shade of green.

---

## Why "local grocery" is a deliberate choice

This course could have said "multi-vendor marketplace" and stopped. It didn't, because **the domain changes real decisions**:

**Units matter.** You don't sell "2 apples" — you sell **2 kg of apples** or **6 pieces**. Every product carries a `unit` enum and a decimal `weightOrQuantity`.

**Stock is not always an integer.** Half a kilo of loose tomatoes is valid inventory. `stockQty` is a number, not necessarily a whole item count.

**Perishability is a first-class flag.** `isPerishable` exists on the product because grocery is not furniture — it informs how you think about fulfilment, delivery windows, and the bar-raiser feature you might add later.

**Photos are load-bearing.** Produce is sold with the eyes. Image storage strategy (object storage, not MongoDB blobs) shows up early, not as a late polish item.

**Multi-vendor cart is the core tension.** One checkout, many shops — the split, the snapshots, the per-vendor order documents — that's the architectural heart of the course.

If you swapped "grocery" for "generic goods" and nothing in the schema changed, the domain would be a label, not a design force. Here it is a design force.

## The two actors

Every feature in the course serves one of these two people — or the relationship between them.

### The vendor — a local shop owner

**Who they are:** Someone who runs a grocery shop, farm stall, or specialty food store. They are not an employee of the platform. They are an **independent seller** using your marketplace the way a seller uses Etsy or Amazon Marketplace — except the domain is food, not crafts.

**What they need from the product:**

- Register and prove they own their email before they can open a shop
- Create **one store profile** — name, description, address, logo
- Manage **their own product catalogue** — add, edit, remove listings; set price, unit, weight, stock, perishable flag
- Upload **product photos** that load fast and don't bloat the database
- See **only their incoming orders** — never another vendor's customers or revenue
- Update order **fulfilment status** as they pack and dispatch — pending → packed → dispatched

**What they must never be able to do:**

- View or edit another vendor's store or products
- See another vendor's orders
- Create a second store on the same account (one shop per vendor, enforced by the API)

**How it should feel:** Like a **shop back office** — plain, functional, focused. A table of products with edit and delete. A list of today's orders. No marketplace-wide admin view. When a vendor logs in, the world is **their shop**, not the whole platform database.

---

### The customer — a shopper

**Who they are:** Anyone buying groceries from local vendors through the platform. They might shop from one store or five in a single session.

**What they need from the product:**

- Register and log in as a **customer** (distinct role from vendor)
- **Browse** the full marketplace catalogue — all vendors, filterable by category and shop
- **Search** products by name; paginate results without loading the entire collection
- Add items from **multiple vendors** into **one cart**
- **Check out once** — the platform splits into per-vendor orders automatically
- View **order history** with the prices they actually paid, unchanged if the vendor updates listings later

**What they must never be able to do:**

- See or modify another customer's cart or orders
- Create or edit products (that's vendor role)
- Access vendor-only routes

**How it should feel:** Like a **clean editorial shop** — photography-forward product cards, clear price with unit ("₹120 / kg"), store name visible on each card, cart grouped by shop so the upcoming split isn't a surprise at checkout.

---

## There is no platform admin in this course

Deliberately. An admin panel — banning users, moderating listings, viewing all orders — is **out of scope** (see the next sub-chapter). The course focuses on the **two-sided marketplace tension**: vendor isolation and customer commerce. That's where the hard engineering lives.

If you finish early, the bar-raiser might invite you to add a minimal admin role — but the core course does not depend on it.

## How the two actors meet — the core flows

### Flow 1 — Vendor onboarding

```
Register as vendor → verify email (OTP) → log in →
  no store yet? → open your shop (name, address, description) →
  add products (price, unit, stock, photo) → publish →
  wait for orders
```

The course builds this across Week 2. By the end, you demo it in the browser — not just in Postman.

### Flow 2 — Customer purchase

```
Register as customer → log in → browse catalogue →
  filter / search → add to cart (items from Shop A and Shop B) →
  review cart (grouped by store) → checkout →
  platform creates Order A for Shop A, Order B for Shop B →
  customer sees order history; each vendor sees their slice
```

The course builds this across Week 3. Checkout is the chapter where **price snapshots** and **order splitting** come together — the canonical marketplace problems.

### Flow 3 — What happens when a vendor changes a price

This is worth stating explicitly because it drives a core design decision:

1. Customer bought 1 kg rice at **₹80** last Tuesday
2. Vendor updates rice to **₹95** this Monday
3. Customer opens order history

**Correct behaviour:** Order history still shows **₹80** — what they paid, frozen at checkout.

**Wrong behaviour:** Order history shows **₹95** — because the order line only stored `productId` and reads live price.

The course teaches **why** the wrong behaviour happens before you build the right one — and you build the right one from the start in the checkout chapter.

## Roles — one account, one role

Each user account carries a **role**: `vendor` or `customer`. Registration sets it; the JWT carries it; middleware enforces it.

| Action | Customer | Vendor |
|---|---|---|
| Browse public catalogue | ✓ | ✓ |
| Manage own cart & checkout | ✓ | ✓ (as buyer — vendors can shop too) |
| Create / edit products | ✗ | ✓ (own only) |
| Open a store | ✗ | ✓ (one store) |
| View vendor order dashboard | ✗ | ✓ (own orders only) |

**Authentication** answers *who* is calling. **Authorization** answers *what they're allowed to do* — and, for vendors, *whether this specific store or product is theirs*. You'll build both, and you'll test Vendor B attacking Vendor A's IDs.

## The ownership chain — preview

Every piece of data belongs to someone. The course returns to this chain repeatedly:

```
User (vendor role)
  └── Store (one per vendor)
        └── Product
              └── referenced by CartItem (customer's cart)
              └── snapshotted into OrderItem (at checkout)

User (customer role)
  └── CartItem → Product → Store
  └── Order (one per vendor at checkout) → OrderItems (snapshots)
```

If you can trace any product back to its store and that store back to its vendor user, isolation checks have a clear path. If you can't, every authorization decision becomes guesswork.

You'll draw this as an ERD in Week 1 and refine it as features arrive.

## Grocery-specific product shape — preview

When you model products in Chapter 5, each listing includes at minimum:

| Field | Example | Why |
|---|---|---|
| `name` | Basmati Rice | Display |
| `price` | 120 | Per unit below |
| `unit` | `kg` / `g` / `litre` / `piece` | Grocery sells by measure |
| `weightOrQuantity` | 1 | Default pack size or increment |
| `stockQty` | 45.5 | Decimal stock |
| `isPerishable` | false | Domain flag |
| `category` | Grains | Browse filter |
| `imageUrl` | Cloudinary URL | Photo, not blob |
| `storeId` | ref → Store | Ownership |

A generic marketplace might omit half of these. This one can't — and that's intentional.

## What the product is not

Stating negatives sharply prevents gold-plating the wrong features:

- **Not a payment processor** — orders are placed; payment is simulated or "pending"
- **Not a delivery tracker** — status goes to "dispatched"; no GPS, no courier API
- **Not a review site** — no stars, no comments on products
- **Not real-time chat** — vendors and customers don't message in-app
- **Not an admin SaaS** — no platform operator dashboard in scope

Those exclusions are freedom. They keep four weeks focused on the problems that define a marketplace: **identity, isolation, catalogue, cart, checkout, deploy**.

## Picture the finished demo

Before you write code, picture the screen recording you'll make at the end — two minutes, both flows:

**Minute one — vendor:** Log in as a vendor → open shop if needed → create "Organic Honey" at ₹450/kg with a photo → see it in the product table → log out.

**Minute two — customer:** Log in as customer → browse → add honey from that vendor and rice from another → cart shows two store groups → checkout → order confirmation shows **two orders** → change honey's price in the vendor dashboard → customer order history still shows ₹450.

If your final app can do that, you've built the product this chapter described. Every chapter between here and there serves that demo.

## Key ideas from this page

- The product is a **local grocery marketplace** — one cart, many vendors, split checkout
- **Two actors:** vendor (shop back office) and customer (editorial shop front)
- The **domain** forces units, decimals, perishables, and photos — not optional fields
- **Ownership chain:** User → Store → Product → Cart / Order
- **Price snapshots** protect order history when vendors change prices
- No admin role in scope — the tension is vendor ↔ customer ↔ platform logic
