# 1.3 — The product, and the people in it

Before you build a system, you have to understand the *thing* — then the people who use it. Let's meet both.

## The product

Your platform is a home for **communities**. Anyone can create one — `r/woodworking`, `r/localhistory`, `r/typescript`, whatever — give it a name and a description, and it becomes a space others can join.

Inside a community, members **post** — a title, some text, maybe an image or a link. Each post opens into a **discussion**: a thread of **comments**, where a comment can reply to the post *or to another comment*, branching into nested conversations that can run deep.

Everything is shaped by **votes**. Members vote posts and comments up or down, and those votes drive **ranking** — the platform decides what's "hot" right now, what's "top" of all time, and what's simply "new". A good post in a small community can rise; a bad one sinks. That ranking, applied to your joined communities, becomes your personalized **home feed** — the first thing you see when you log in.

And because open communities attract bad actors, every community has **moderators** who keep it healthy: removing rule-breaking content, locking heated threads, pinning announcements, and banning the people who won't behave.

A clean, readable, text-first style suits this — think calm and legible, not loud. You'll make the visual decisions; the course cares that the *system* is right.

## The people (actors)

Four kinds of people use the platform. Understanding what each one *needs* tells you what to build and what to protect.

### Visitor (anonymous)

Someone not logged in. They can **browse** public communities and read posts and comments — discovery should work without an account, the way it does on Reddit. But they can't post, comment, vote, or join. The moment they try, you invite them to sign up.

### Member (registered user)

The core actor. A member can **join communities**, **create posts**, **write comments**, **vote**, and build a small identity (a username, a profile). Most of the platform exists to serve members well — and most of your security exists to make sure a member can only touch *their own* content and the communities they belong to.

### Moderator

A member with extra power **within a specific community** they help run. A moderator can **remove** posts and comments, **lock** or **pin** threads, and **ban** members from that community. Crucially, their power is *scoped to their community* — a mod of `r/woodworking` has no authority in `r/typescript`. Getting that scoping right is a real part of the course.

### Admin (platform-wide)

A trusted operator with authority across the whole platform — handling site-wide abuse, removing communities that break the rules. You'll keep this role small and deliberate; most day-to-day moderation belongs to community moderators, not admins.

---

Hold these four in mind: nearly every feature you build will ask *"who's allowed to do this — a visitor, a member, a mod of this community, or an admin?"* That question is the spine of the whole system. Next, let's draw the line around exactly what you will and won't build.
