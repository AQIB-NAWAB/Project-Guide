# 1.2 — How to use this course

This is not a tutorial you copy-paste. It's a **guided build**: it teaches you each idea, then walks you through implementing it *on your own project*, step by step — and it expects you to write the actual code yourself. That's the whole point. Copying produces nothing you can repeat; building with understanding produces an engineer.

Read this section carefully — it's how everything below works.

## The shape: phases → chapters → sub-chapters

The course is organized in three levels:

- **Phases** (four of them, one per week) group the work into big stages: foundations → identity & interaction → feeds & media → real-time & ship.
- **Chapters** are whole **feature areas** — *Authentication*, *Comments*, *Voting & ranking*, *Real-time updates*. Each chapter is a milestone: when you finish it, a real part of your platform works.
- **Sub-chapters** are the small, guided **steps** inside a chapter. A feature chapter has many of them — *set the scene → learn the concept → make a decision → then build it, one file at a time*. You complete them in order, in a sitting each.

So you're never handed a wall of "go build authentication." You're handed twenty small steps that *add up to* authentication, each one telling you exactly what to do next.

## Every chapter teaches, then builds

Each chapter follows the same rhythm:

1. **Set the scene** — what this feature is, why it matters, and a real story to ground it.
2. **Learn it** — the concept, the options, and *which* approach this project uses and **why** (with the trade-offs laid out).
3. **Read** — a short list of **mandatory reads** that the build assumes.
4. **Build it** — the hands-on part: *create this folder, create this file, here's what it must do, here's what to avoid, now test it.* Each step continues from the last, so your project grows in front of you.
5. **Checklist** — proof the feature works.

Along the way you'll get **hints** when a step is tricky, **✅ do / ❌ don't** notes to steer you clear of common mistakes, and the occasional true war story from how real teams solved the same problem.

## The checklist is a gate

This is the most important rule in the course: **every chapter ends in a checklist, and the checklist is a gate.**

You do **not** move to the next chapter until *every box is ticked* — until you can see or demo each item working. The checklist is your permission to proceed, and the thing that keeps you honest in a self-paced course. Skipping it just means the next chapter builds on something that isn't really there.

## Keep a learning log (mandatory)

In your project repo, create a folder called **`learning-log/`**. At the end of each chapter you'll be asked a few questions — about the decisions you made and the concepts you used. **Write your answers in a file there** (`learning-log/06-authentication.md`, and so on), and commit them.

This is **not optional.** Those written answers are:

- the **evidence** you actually cleared the gate (not just skimmed it),
- your **rehearsal** for the final viva,
- and what a mentor (or an AI reviewer) reads to check your understanding.

If you can't answer a chapter's questions in your own words, you haven't finished the chapter — go back.

## How you're judged

On **understanding**, at a final walkthrough (a viva): you'll open any part of your platform and explain *how it works and what would break it*. The per-chapter checklists and your learning log are how you stay ready for it. You may use AI to help you build — but the viva checks that *you* can explain your own work.

## When you're stuck

In order: **read the error message** (really read it), **read the docs**, **try a smaller version** to isolate the problem, and only then ask — framed as *"here's what I expected, here's what actually happened."* That framing alone solves half of all problems.
